Why CISOs Burn Out: The Unseen Toll of a Life on Cybersecurity's Front Line

In the high-stakes world of cybersecurity, the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) sits at the eye of the storm. Tasked with defending their organization against a relentless and ever-evolving wave of cyber threats, CISOs carry a weight few outside the profession truly understand. With constant pressure, sleepless nights, and the knowledge that a single mistake could cost millions — or even lives — it's no wonder many CISOs face extreme stress. Alarmingly, it’s becoming increasingly clear: the job may be cutting lives short.

A 24/7 Crisis Mode

The modern CISO doesn’t clock out at 5 PM. Cyberattacks don’t respect time zones, holidays, or weekends. Whether it’s ransomware, phishing, DDoS attacks, or zero-day vulnerabilities, CISOs are always on high alert.

“There’s no downtime. I sleep with my phone next to me, hoping it doesn’t buzz at 3 a.m.” — Veteran CISO at a Fortune 500 company

The result? Chronic stress, anxiety, insomnia — all of which contribute to long-term health issues like hypertension, heart disease, and burnout.

Lonely at the Top

Despite their crucial role, CISOs often operate in isolation. They’re caught between executive leadership, who may not fully understand cybersecurity, and technical teams looking for direction. When breaches occur, fingers are quick to point at the CISO — even when the failure is organizational.

This scapegoat status is emotionally taxing. CISOs often report feeling unsupported, misunderstood, or even disposable.

The Invisible War

Unlike other business functions, cybersecurity is largely a “no-win” game. Success is invisible — no breach, no headlines, no applause. But failure? It’s a front-page scandal.

This psychological asymmetry means CISOs rarely get recognition for doing their jobs well, but are the first to fall when things go wrong. This constant pressure to be perfect — with no glory — takes a silent toll.

Short Tenure, High Turnover

The average tenure of a CISO is shockingly low — around 18–26 months. Many either resign voluntarily due to burnout or are forced out after a breach. This revolving door doesn’t just hurt organizations; it destabilizes the mental and physical well-being of those in the role.

Why Some Don’t Make It

It's a grim topic, but one that needs confronting: a growing number of CISOs suffer serious health consequences — including early mortality — due to chronic stress and its cascading effects on the body.

High cortisol levels. Constant adrenaline. Poor diet from long hours. Little time for family or hobbies. It’s a recipe for physical breakdown. Some CISOs have died suddenly from heart attacks, strokes, or other stress-related conditions. While this isn’t unique to cybersecurity, the unrelenting nature of the role amplifies the danger.

How to Change the Story

Organizations must begin treating cybersecurity as a shared responsibility — not a CISO’s burden alone. Here’s how:

  • Mental health support: Therapy, stress management programs, and wellness initiatives.
  • Executive buy-in: Cybersecurity shouldn’t be a silo — leadership must prioritize and understand its complexity.
  • Balanced expectations: CISOs need time off, manageable workloads, and support systems like any other executive.
  • Shared accountability: Security is a company-wide concern, not one person’s job.

Final Thoughts

CISOs are digital guardians — the ones standing between organizations and catastrophic loss. But who protects them?

Until businesses recognize the human cost of cybersecurity leadership, we risk losing our most dedicated professionals not just to resignation — but to something far more tragic.
